Some Stats of Ice and Fire

2014-04-06

Have you ever been watching Game of Thrones and then wondered to yourself, "What if I watched every episode, wrote down all the dialogue in some scripts, generated some statistics on how much each character spoke for each episode, and then turned that information into an online dashboard?" If you're most people, the answer is probably no, but as I was re-watching the episodes in anticipation of the upcoming season, this very idea struck me. Anyway, I found some interesting patterns in the data on my own, which I've written about below, but I encourage you to check out the dashboard here so you can compare character dialogue yourself. Both the below post and the dashboard contain MASSIVE SPOILERS, so if you're not caught up, I'd recommend not reading any further. In addition, if you have any further feedback or more analyses you'd like to see, feel free to email me at mcschorer@gmail.com.

Also, if you're wondering how does Ned have dialogue in season 3 when he didn't have a head by that point, the answer is that Bran has a dream in the second episode of the season, and in that dream, he hears Ned's voice.

GoT Has Had More Lannister Dialogue Than Stark Dialogue Since the 2nd Season

Considering the top 10 list by character wasn't what I expected, I also looked at the percentage of dialogue by house at the series progressed, which is below (I made a lot of simplifying assumptions, so take these numbers with a grain of salt). This chart looks at the cumulative average of dialogue attributable to the major houses as the show progressed.

In my head, I always saw the Starks as the central family in the story, with the Targaryens, Baratheons, and Lannisters playing more supporting roles. If we look at the show based on dialogue, though, I was definitely wrong. In fact, except for the Lannisters, it appears that the proportion of dialogue for each of the major hours is steadily declining. I think this trend is mainly due to the inclusion of multiple other subplots (ex: NW/Wildlings, Tyrells, Greyjoys, Varys/Baelish, etc.), and there's only so much dialogue that can happen in one hour.


Hodor.

I had to count the number of times Hodor says "Hodor". I noticed that he's increased from 3 times in the first season to 5 times in the second and 18 times in the third. Although it's a small sample size, one could argue this is exponential growth. Assuming Hodor continues to increase his Hodoring at an exponential trend, and Tyrion continue to average the same amount of dialogue in the following seasons, we see that Hodor would hypothetically catch Tyrion around Season 9 (although the show will likely only be 7-8 seasons).

With that said, if Hodor's presence increases at this rate, this possibly confirms a common fan theory: Hodor is indeed the Prince That was Promised and the true claimant to the Iron Throne.
